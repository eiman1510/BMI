Body Mass Index (BMI) is a measure used to assess an individual's body weight relative to their height. It is calculated by dividing a person's weight in kilograms by the square of their height in meters. BMI is widely used as a screening tool to categorize individuals into various weight categories such as underweight, normal weight, overweight, and obesity. It provides a simple, quick assessment of potential weight-related health risks. However, BMI does not account for muscle mass, bone density, or fat distribution, which can affect its accuracy in some cases.

The Body Mass Index (BMI) ranges categorize individuals based on their BMI values. A BMI less than 18.5 is considered underweight. A BMI between 18.5 and 24.9 is classified as normal or healthy weight. A BMI between 25 and 29.9 is categorized as overweight. A BMI of 30 or above is classified as obese, with further subcategories such as class I (30-34.9), class II (35-39.9), and class III (40 and above), which indicate increasing levels of obesity severity.

Body Mass Index (BMI) is an important measure that assesses an individual's body weight relative to their height, providing a quick indication of potential health risks. High BMI values are linked to increased risks of heart disease, diabetes, and other chronic conditions, while low BMI can signal malnutrition and other health issues. By serving as a useful screening tool, BMI enables early detection and intervention, helping individuals and healthcare providers monitor and manage weight-related health concerns effectively. Though not perfect, it offers a standardized metric for guiding health and public health policies.
